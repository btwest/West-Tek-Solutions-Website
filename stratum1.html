Mission Description

Mission: Stratum

This mission is about applying and testing our 3D printing capabilities in a more conventional model rocket. Unlike the GNAT, which was a simple two-piece tumble-recovery design requiring minimal assembly, the Stratum is a more complex eight-piece rocket. Its name reflects the layered construction that defines both its design and its assembly process. Every step—from fitting the individual 3D-printed components to executing a controlled launch and recovery—is an opportunity to refine our fabrication techniques and gain hands-on familiarity with conventional model rocketry. This mission combines material experimentation with practical experience, ensuring that each launch not only tests the limits of our prints but also strengthens the foundational knowledge we’ll build upon for future rockets.

Technical Specifications

  • Engine: Estes B6-4 Single-Use Motor
  • Estimated Altitude: 800 feet
  • Recovery: Parachute
  • Payload: None

Mission Objectives

🎯 Primary Objective

3D print, assemble, launch, and recover a larger, more conventional model rocket.

🔬 Secondary Objective

Test recovery methods, including different parachute sizes and parachutes with vent holes, to evaluate descent control and reliability.

📚 Experience Objective

Gain additional hands-on experience with rocket assembly, flight procedures, and recovery operations to build practical rocketry skills.

Mission Summary

SUCCESS

West-Tek Solutions successfully completed our inaugural rocket launch on September 7, 2025, under partly cloudy skies with temperatures in the mid to high 60s°F and relatively windy conditions. We launched four GNAT 1 rockets, each powered by 1/2 A3-4T motors, with all four achieving successful liftoffs and flight profiles. Most importantly, we successfully recovered our very first launch vehicle in perfect condition—a fantastic omen for West-Tek's entry into rocketry.

While we only recovered one of the four rockets (a 25% recovery rate), this actually represents an impressive achievement given that GNAT rockets are infamous for being difficult to recover. The recovered GNAT 1 showed no meaningful structural damage, with the PLA plastic construction performing excellently under flight stresses. Given its historic significance as our first successful launch and recovery, we've decided to retire this particular vehicle for posterity.

Overall, we're extremely happy with this launch and learned tremendous amounts about the entire process. The primary objective of GNAT 1 wasn't just to fly a rocket, but to master everything that goes into launching rockets—from pre-flight preparation and safety protocols to recovery operations and post-flight analysis. Through this mission, we've established our operational procedures, validated our 3D printing techniques under real-world conditions, and built the institutional knowledge that comes only from hands-on experience. Because of everything we learned during GNAT 1, we feel strongly positioned and ready to tackle future launches of bigger and better rockets.

Results

🚀 Launch Performance

Four consecutive successful launches with clean liftoffs at T+0 for all vehicles. No misfires or delays encountered. All GNAT 1 rockets demonstrated excellent flight characteristics and stability throughout their flight profiles.

📏 Actual Altitude

Estimated 400 feet across all launches, meeting expected performance parameters for 1/2 A3-4T motor configuration.

🪂 Recovery

1 of 4 rockets successfully recovered (25% recovery rate) - an impressive achievement for GNAT-class vehicles. The recovered rocket was in stellar condition with no structural damage, validating our 3D-printed PLA construction.

📝 Lessons Learned

Key insights include supply chain optimization needs, recovery method improvements requiring faster descent rates, and the critical importance of higher visibility systems for tracking and recovery operations in future missions.
